# Learning format

A Learning pitches a tool or process worth adopting. It's the deep artifact — write it to stand on its own.

## Headline

`Tool or process: benefit-driven action` — sentence case, 5–10 words, active voice.

- Good: "Atlassian Projects: your best work deserves better than Slack"
- Avoid: "An overview of Atlassian Projects and its features"

## The beats

Cover these in tight prose, not a numbered list:

1. **Pain point** — what's broken or missing today.
2. **Solution** — how this tool or process fixes it, and the specific mechanism.
3. **Adoption cost** — what teams do differently, honest about the friction.
4. **Transformation** — what this unlocks (real-time vs. retrospective, proactive vs. reactive).

## Optional sections

Add only when they earn their place:

- **Known constraints** — 2–4 bullets, each under 15 words. Limitations stakeholders should know up front. Frame as "things to know", not "reasons not to do this". Skip deal-breakers — those are Risks.
- **Next steps** — 3–5 bullets, ordered validation → preparation → pilot → measurement. Name who approves, what to build, how to test, what to measure.
- **The bet** — the timeline commitment, with confidence.

## Worked example

> # Learning: Atlassian Projects: your best work deserves better than Slack
>
> Every great grassroots project at Greenhouse starts the same way — a burst of energy, a dedicated Slack channel, a flurry of decisions that exist nowhere else. Then the channel goes quiet and that knowledge just... evaporates. Glean helps recover some of it, but hunting for answers in stale messages isn't the same as having a source of truth you can trust. Atlassian Projects gives grassroots work a real home — structured updates, visible progress and decisions that don't require an archaeology dig to find. If you're already in Jira, there's zero new tooling to justify; this is just a better place to put work that deserves to be seen. The grassroots energy here is genuinely one of our superpowers — let's build infrastructure worthy of it.

Why it works: opens on a pain everyone recognizes, names the mechanism, kills the adoption objection ("zero new tooling"), and closes on what it unlocks — all in flowing prose. Known constraints and next steps were left off because the pitch didn't need them; add them when they sharpen the case.
